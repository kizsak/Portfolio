Simulated Polaroid

Digital image, 2026
I generated a synthetic photograph of an Ophrys apifera (Bee Orchid) using Midjourney. I then wrote a Python script (download) that algorithmically simulates material decay by introducing staged degradations—edge diffusion, tonal compression, color drift, and localized light artifacts. Rather than applying a single filter, the script executes these transformations sequentially, allowing each computational layer to interfere with and modify the previous one.

Graffiti Sunrise

Digital image, 2025
I created a large library (1,000+) individually generated mark-making elements created on transparent backgrounds. I used dozens of brush, smear, scrape, and texture variations as standalone PNG files. I then took a photograph I had taken of a sunset, and begin to digitally collage the marks into the frame, roughly using the strata and color palette created in the original photograph as the primary compositional elements in the finished piece. I digitally layered the marks I had made, arranging them in horizontal bands to establish a gradient shift from warm oranges and reds to cooler violets and blues. Rather than blending with a brush tool, I relied on opacity adjustments, layering order, and overlap to create transitions between color zones. The glow at the horizon emerges through density — more concentrated, brighter marks placed lower in the composition — while upper areas become increasingly sparse and cooler in tone. The final image is the result of cumulative layering, transparency control, and careful color sequencing across thousands of individual elements.

Fractured Self, 2025

Digital image, 2025
I began with a selfie. I ran it through an AI tool that identified the hex codes that were prominent in my image–basically, my personal color palette. Next, I mixed those hex codes with prompts focused on my favorite flowers and created 700 images of botanical photographs on Midjourney in my skin, hair, and eye colors. Then, I wrote a Python script to remove the background of all of the images, so they all became cut-out flowers. Then, I hand-placed them into a digital collage, using my original selfie as a reference.

Sometimes I Fall Apart I

Digital image, 2025
Process notes: I began with a photographic portrait. I then created 700+ botanical images on solid color backgrounds, using Midjourney. I then processed them using a Python photomosaic script (download), to divide the image into a fine grid. The script then algorithmically replaced each square, with an appropriately colored image from the image dataset. The script analyzed average RGB values of each tile in the base image and matched them to the closest corresponding image tile from the dataset, constructing the portrait as a composite of hundreds of micro-images. The result is a dual-resolution system: from a distance, the image resolves into a coherent face; up close, it fractures into a dense archive of individual pictures. The computational structure becomes visible as a patterned lattice across the skin, foregrounding the underlying grid logic rather than concealing it. After generating the mosaic output, I digitally painted over portions of the image to reintroduce gestural brushwork—particularly in the hair—creating tension between algorithmic assembly and painterly intervention. This layering emphasizes the dialogue between procedural construction (script-driven tile replacement) and expressive manual mark-making. The work explores how identity can be rendered as a dataset: constructed from fragments, aggregated through rules, and perceptually unified through scale.

Sometimes I Fall Apart II

Digital image, 2025
Process notes: I began with a photograph of myself and my husband at an installation at Color Factory NYC. I then utilized HxD--a hexadecimal editor--to break down the image into raw binary data and manually modify portions of its underlying byte structure. I selectively changed, overwrote, and inserted data in the file’s hex code (while preserving enough of the header to keep the file readable), I introduced structural corruption into the image format itself. When reopened as an image, these disruptions manifested as horizontal banding, color channel shifts, and strip-based misalignment. The repeating green and purple bars are not painted overlays but artifacts of file-level intervention — the visual trace of altered compression or encoding instructions. The accident of one band spelling out "A Lot of Pizza" makes the image like it has something compelling to say about human life.

Episodic Disability

Digital image, 2025
Process notes: I began with a photographic self-portrait and increased tonal contrast to emphasize facial structure. Using Posterize and Gradient Map layers in Photoshop, I reduced the image to discrete tonal bands and remapped them to a high-contrast red, yellow, black, and cyan palette. The animated GIF was created in Photoshop’s Timeline by shifting tonal thresholds across frames, producing a flickering instability that reflects the work’s episodic theme and captures the experience of a chronic illness flare.

The Measure of a Virus

Digital image, 2026
Process notes: This piece began as a reconstruction of Leonardo da Vinci’s Vitruvian Man—a system built on idealized male proportion and geometric order. I worked with Midjourney to create a drawing of the Vitruvian Man, then asked it to do a drawing of my face in the style of the original Rennaissance drawing. I used Photoshop to insert my head and adjust the figure's limbs to more female proportions. Next, I hand-drew layer masks, frame-by-frame, to preserve a tactile, hand-intervened quality. The motion is minimal but perceptible: the body flickers between historical archetype and contemporary insertion. Conceptually, the work is intended to capture the experience of Long COVID--new body parts failing practically daily.
