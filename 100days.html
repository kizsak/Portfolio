x Kate Izsak — #100DayProject

#100DayProject2026

The 100 Day Project is a creative practice in which artists commit to producing a small work every day for one hundred days. The project originated in a Yale School of Art assignment by designer Michael Bierut. To participate, an artist must choose a creative action and repeat it daily for 100 days, emphasizing process, experimentation, and sustained creative attention rather than polished outcomes.

I am using the 2026 100 Day Project--which began on Feburary 18, 2026--to work through a particular haze of grief. I live with a cocktail of ailments: Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome, Mast Cell Activation Syndrome, Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome. Toss in a primary immunodeficiency (that makes me a vaccine non-responder) and, since March 2020, the unrelenting specter of long COVID. COVID hit me early—before the lockdown began—and it hit me hard, fundamentally changing my mind and body and ripping a vast, insurmountable gulf between me and the people I loved. I suffered a particular trauma at the hands of the people I loved most in the world, who stopped speaking to me--in unison--in 2023. I have written about this experience elsewhere, but writing has not helped me reach the level of closure I wish, leaving me still grappling with the fracturing of long-standing friendships after adult-onset disability. In my personal essay (above), I describe the experience of watching long-standing friendships become brittle, develop cracks, then eventually implode.

What remained were hundreds of photographs. These images document ordinary moments of friendship: shared meals, trips, celebrations, casual afternoons. But when I look at them now, the emotional weight of the rupture makes the images difficult to approach. The people who gave those moments meaning are still present in the photographs, but the relationships themselves no longer exist. In an effort to reclaim 20 years of memory, I am removing those friends from the photographs. Using digital and computational editing techniques—cloning, reconstruction, and other forms of image manipulation— I gradually erase their figures from the images. The environments remain: a room, a street, a table, a landscape. The architecture of the memory persists, but the people who once defined it disappear.

The goal is not to pretend these friendships never existed. Instead, the process is about reclaiming the memories themselves. By editing the images, I create a new relationship to them—one that allows me to approach the places, moments, and fragments of those years without being overwhelmed by the loss embedded in the original photographs. Across one hundred days, the project will be a meditation on absence, agency, and digital reconstruction. Each image holds two truths at once: it documents a past moment that cannot be restored, while also becoming a newly constructed space in which memory can be re-entered on different terms.
