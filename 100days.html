x Kate Izsak — Expressive Abstracts

#100DayProject

The 100 Day Project is a creative practice in which artists commit to producing a small work every day for one hundred days. The project originated in a Yale School of Art assignment by designer Michael Bierut and later led by artist Elle Luna and The Great Discontent. The premise is simple: choose an action and repeat it daily for 100 days, emphasizing process, experimentation, and sustained creative attention rather than polished outcomes.
I am using the 2026 100 Day Project to work through a particular haze of grief. In 2023, after I developed multiple chronic illnesses and became immunocompromised during the COVID era, an entire friend group of fourteen people gradually cut me off. In my essay “Left Behind: Disability, Trauma, and Social Isolation in the COVID-19 Era,” I describe the experience of watching long-standing friendships fracture under the pressure of disability, risk perception, and social exclusion. What remained were hundreds of photographs. These images document ordinary moments of friendship: shared meals, trips, celebrations, casual afternoons. But when I look at them now, the emotional weight of the rupture makes the images difficult to approach. The people who gave those moments meaning are still present in the photographs, but the relationships themselves no longer exist. In an effort to reclaim 20 years of memory, I am removing those friends from the photographs. Using digital editing techniques—cloning, reconstruction, and other forms of image manipulation—I gradually erase their figures from the images. The environments remain: a room, a street, a table, a landscape. The architecture of the memory persists, but the people who once defined it disappear. The goal is not to pretend these friendships never existed. Instead, the process is about reclaiming the memories themselves. By editing the images, I create a new relationship to them—one that allows me to approach the places, moments, and fragments of those years without being overwhelmed by the loss embedded in the original photographs. Across one hundred days, the project becomes a meditation on absence, agency, and digital reconstruction. Each image holds two truths at once: it documents a past moment that cannot be restored, while also becoming a newly constructed space in which memory can be re-entered on different terms.
